Visual Content Analysis


By: Meghan Kelly

Execuive Summary

Definition

Content analysis describes the systematic analysis of a corpus of visual artifacts in order to detect quantitative and qualitative patterns and themes from elements within the visuals.


Pros for interactive mapping:

  1. 1. Quantify and examine visual elements within a single interactive map
  2. 2. Compare visual elements across a series of interactive maps
  3. 3. Longitudinally compare changes in map styles and techniques
  4. 4. Identify minute differences within a series of interactive maps
  5. 5. Does not require access to expert participants
  6. 6. Analyze similar or competing interactive maps and tools early in the user design process

Cons for interactive mapping:

  1. 1. Focuses on the composition of visuals, not their designers or users
  2. 2. Does not assess the effectiveness or interpretation of a visual
  3. 3. Does not assess designer’s intention and subjectivity
  4. 4. Does not deconstruct power within visuals
  5. 5. Does not require access to expert participants
  6. 6. Requires time, focus, and potentially more than one researcher for coding process

What?

Description

Visual content analysis is a systematic coding process that analyzes the composition or visual elements within a series of representations, interactive or otherwise. Cartography, particularly interactive cartography, has only recently utilized visual content analysis to decipher and quantify patterns and trends within a sample of maps. Visual content analysis provides a systematic means to analyze large datasets of maps to reveal similarities or dissimilarities within. Researchers develop and implement a series of codes or variables to evaluate across each map. These codes are geared towards particular research questions and draw on established frameworks in map design and representation. For example, common codes for static maps include the map subject or theme, projection, scale, map type, visual variables, color schemes, information density, labeling, and map elements (Kessler and Slocum 2011; White et al. 2017; Fish and Calvert forthcoming). Meanwhile, researchers interested in assessing interactive maps may code for representation, but also interactive functionality or operators like pan, zoom, or popup retrieval capabilities as well as non-functional requirements such as underlying technology, support for mobile, and general usability. Visual content analysis can assess both quantitative and qualitative attributes with these codes. The coding process returns numerical data that can then be analyzed to detect relationships and correlations.

Influential Disciplines

Frequently used to analyze texts (i.e. identifying key words, phrases and themes in an interview) content analysis has been extended beyond textual analysis to visual analysis (Cope 2011 and Rose 2012). As society becomes continually ingrained with visuals, there is an increased demand to understand cultural representations. As such, content analysis has been utilized in the fields of media studies, cultural studies, social science, usability engineering and to some extent, cartography. Media studies, for example, has used content analysis to analyze advertisements over time and cultural theorists have utilized content analysis to examine the depiction of women in National Geographic Magazine (Krippendorf 1989 and Lutz and Collins 1993). Visualization and usability engineers have similarly used content analysis or competitive analysis to explore existing standard applications during the first step of the UI/UX design process (Roth et al. 2015b). While the influences and applications of content analysis are vast, it is important to note the differential goals in its usage. Usability engineers, for example, might utilize content analysis for quick and practical analysis to inform successful interface design at low cost. Alternatively, content analysis in media studies might appraise and critique contemporary visual culture.

Content Analysis in Cartography

Content analysis is well-suited for cartographic analysis given the field’s reliance on visual representations. Ian Muehlenhaus (2011) recognizes the method as a highly underutilized, yet advantageous method for cartography. Several cartographers have used content analysis in their scholarly work on static representations. Muehlenhaus (2011 and 2013) has used visual content analysis to temporally explore minute changes in thematic map design in Goode’s World Atlas editions and to better understand the design and composition of persuasive maps. In the former piece, Muehlenhaus provides a detailed methodological account for future work cartographic work. Similarly, Slocum and Kessler (2011) historically examine thematic map design in two prominent geography journals—Annals of the Association of American Geographers and The Geographical Journal. To better understand cartographic conventions and techniques utilized in contemporary media, Kelly (2015) combines content and discourse analysis to understand the composition and power embedded within maps produced by western media. Kelly (2015) demonstrates the effective use of content analysis in combination with additional visual methodologies like discourse analysis.
More and more, visual content analysis has also been incorporated into interactive mapping research. Fish and Calvert (forthcoming) have recently utilized content analysis to explore alternative energy management and practice in online interactive applications. In their method design, Fish and Calvert expand static map design codes like the visual variables (hue, size, height, texture, etc.) to interaction design codes like interaction operators (pan, zoom, retrieve, sequence, overlay etc.). Roth et al. (2015a) similarly use a competitive analysis or content analysis of climate management applications, specifically management applications related to water, to inform early stages of the design process. Parrott et al. (2007) use content analysis to examine both static and interactive maps published within state comprehensive cancer plans. To further examine interactive functionality, Roth et al. (2015b) use content analysis to examine web mapping technology and open web platforms to inform cartographic higher education.

How to?

Materials

Visual content analysis relies on secondary data and not participants. Because of this, sampling of visual data is increasingly important. Muehlenhaus (2011) notes that random sampling is the best practice. Random sampling, however, is often not realistic because it may not directly answer specified research questions. Convenience and selective sampling can then be strategic even with their subjective limitations. Both sampling techniques can alleviate time and redundancy within a sample. Rose (2012) suggests three additional sampling techniques: stratified, systematic, and cluster. The table below defines and details each sampling method. The most important consideration, according to Muehlenhaus (2011), is transparency and acknowledgement of your sampling methods as well as its advantages and limitations.

Sampling Methods Definition
Random Determine the number of maps needed and then use a random number generator to select final maps (Rose 2012)
Convenience Select maps that are easily accessible and readily available (Muehlenhaus 2011)
Selective Subjectively select maps based on defined research questions and purpose of study (Muehlenhaus 2011)
Stratified Sample from predetermined subgroups (Rose 2012)
Systematic Choose maps based on a predetermined interval, every fifth map (Rose 2012)
Cluster Choose groups at random and sample maps from each group (Rose 2012)
